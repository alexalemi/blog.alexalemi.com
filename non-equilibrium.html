Non-equilibrium Thermodynamics Results Seemingly from Nothing

Alexander A. Alemi. 2022-09-16

Let's see if we can very quickly prove the Jarzynski Equality and related non-equilibrium statistical mechanics results. Much like the mathematical underpinnings of thermodynamics are pretty mathematically simple, e.g. the existence of a convex surface on which mixed partial derivatives commute, I believe most of the results in non-equilibrium statistical mechanics are similarly due to a rhetorical reinterpretation of a simple mathematical manipulation.

This post will assume some familiarity with physics.

Density Ratios

To generate the classic non-equilibrium statistical mechanics results we start by considering a simple ratio of two joint probability distributions: ùëû(ùë•0,ùë•1)ùëù(ùë•0,ùë•1) Clearly we have a tremendous freedom here in our choices for the distributions ùëù and ùëû. Mathematically it's uninteresting but we can start to build some rhetorical weight by factoring our two distributions in two distinct ways: ùëû(ùë•1)ùëû(ùë•0|ùë•1)ùëù(ùë•0)ùëù(ùë•1|ùë•0) Despite still not having done anything, we can start to build an interpretation here. Imagine ùë•0 and ùë•1 as being two configurations of a system, with ùë•1 happening after ùë•0. Now, though we're allowed by the chain rule to factor distributions any way we wish, here we've chosen to factor ùëù to be suggestive of some kind of forward process wherein we first sample some ùë•0 from a distribution ùëù(ùë•0) and then evolve it according to some potentially stochastic process to generate our next state ùë•1 conditioned on the first: ùëù(ùë•1|ùë•0). At the same time, we've factored ùëû the other way, evocative of a reverse process that starts at ùë•1 and then evolves backward to ùë•0.

To make further progress, let's specialize a bit. Let's imagine that ùë•0 and ùë•1 are configurations of a physical system evolving according to Hamiltonian dynamics, with a Hamiltonian governed by some kind of control parameter ùúÜ. Let's further imagine that at the beginning of either our forward or reverse process our system is in thermodynamic equilibrium at the same temperature, and in particular in a canonical ensemble:2

Hamiltonian Dynamics

First, if we assume that our dynamics is Hamiltonian, and thus deterministic and reversible, we know that the probability that we start at ùë•0 and end up at ùë•1 if we evolve forward in time is the same as the probability that we start at ùë•1 and end up at ùë•0 if we reverse our time evolution, (ùëû(ùë•0|ùë•1) =ùëù(ùë•1|ùë•0))3

Consider ùëù the forward, Hamiltonian evolution of a system from ùë•0 to ùë•1 and ùëû the reverse process. The power of this result is that it allows us to relate an expectation computed with respect to non-equilibrium processes (the exponential of the beta weighted stochastic work needed for a bunch of non-equilibrium realizations of our trajectory) to a pure equilibrium quantity (a difference of equilibrium free energies). In the context of the physical sciences, this lets us perform non-equilibrium simulations or experiments, and provided we measure the work performed over many such runs, even with the system driven far from equilibrium, we can estimate equilibrium free energy differences.

Stochastic Dynamics

But, let's say you don't like the assumption that the dynamics are Hamiltonian, we can imagine something else, imagine our dynamics is stochastic but imagine discretizing the dynamics. Variational Autoencoder

To show some of the generality of what we're doing here, let's do it again but for a completely different kind of system, this time a Variational Autoencoder. In a variational autoencoder there are two joint distributions at play, one a representational model ùëù(ùë•,ùëß) =ùëù(ùë•)ùëù(ùëß|ùë•) which starts with a draw from some true data distribution ùëù(ùë•) and then uses an encoder to map that datum to some kind of representative code, or summary, or representation ùëß: ùëù(ùëß|ùë•). The other joint distribution consists of a generative model ùëû(ùë•,ùëß) =ùëû(ùëß)ùëû(ùë•|ùëß) that imagines a joint distribution over the same space but works in reverse. Conclusion

We've managed to derive several non-equilibrium statistical mechanical equalities and inequalities seemingly from nothing. All of these results were powered by the facts we opened with, that probability distributions integrate to one and that KL divergences are positive. The only challenge here was one of semantics. To get power out of such trivial mathematical manipulations required us to make judicious choices in how we interpreted them.
